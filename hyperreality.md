# Hyperreality

Plato discussed the 'ideal forms' of which our perception of reality was an approximation. In hyperreality, it is assumed that our approaches become detached from ideal forms, leaving our perceptions of reality based on other approaches, without the ability to restore the original true form. A multitude of dogmas stem from corruption in science and philosophy, which affect psychology, economics, and culture. On a psychological level, individuals are forced to conform to lies and to deny themselves until their authenticity becomes irrelevant, their individuality becomes annoying and the truth no longer has a voice. 

### The most important notice about our Hyper-reality is the possibility of losing sight of the horizon of reality in a world intertwined with so many signs that only refer to concepts anchored in a virtual world and offered to us 24/7 in real time.

When someone has lost all sense of reality they easily become subject to psychological oppression. This is why it is extremely important to understand hyperreality.

Hyperreality is the inability to distinguish reality from a simulation of reality. It is a term used in postmodern philosophy and has been described by many brilliant modern day philosophers. Jean Baudrillard was first to use the world hyperreality and describe its meaning. Other theorists have modified and added to Baidrillard’s definition. Some have evaluated the effects of hyperreality and the causes behind it. This post in particular will explore the role hyperreality has in both media and news.

Often times in media, a story can be presented that does not entirely reflect actual reality. This happens in a number of formats. TV and film often present a picture of reality that does not actually correspond to what is real. This is especially true in ‘reality TV’. Due to this ‘simulation’ of reality viewers start to believe that the reality depicted in these formats represent true reality. From this, a blur emerges from what is actually true and from what is a simulation of reality. Films that employ CGI to digitally create settings or modify and enhance characters and their features cause audiences to be unable to distinguish between what is actually real and what is not real. This occurs especially in magazines. Digitally enhanced people populate magazine covers and readers of magazines start to believe that what they see on the cover of the magazine is real. A common effect of this hyperreality is that women cannot distinguish between the actual looks of individuals and their enhanced looks, and that they set out to achieve a ‘beauty’ that is physically impossible.

There have been many modern philosophies who have attempted to explain why hyperreality exists. The Italian theorist, Umberto Eco, cites the source of hyperreality is people’s desire for actual reality. In order to achieve actual reality, people either fabricate a false reality themselves or buy into an already fabricated reality. Stemming from their desire for actual reality, people consume this false reality as if it were real. This consumption leads to the blurred lines between what is real and what is only a simulation of reality. As seen by the example of cover magazines, hyperreality can a have strong influence on people. From their lack of distinguish, people act and feel differently.

Umberto Eco - Travels in hyperreality

Media has a distinct role in this process. Medias create a false reality which people who are looking for actual reality consume. Many times, different Medias benefit financially from providing false realities to serve to people because the desire for actual reality is so strong. The lucrative implications of hyperreality can explain the insurgence of ‘reality’ TV programming and digitally enhanced magazine covers. The lucrative implications of hyperreality also effect the type of news that is delivered to people. Media outlets that deliver news content, especially, 24 hour news stations portray news in a way that differs from reality. This does not necessarily mean that facts are distorted or changed from what is real, but rather the way the news is presented does not directly align with reality.

The best examples:
  - the news coverage of the O.J. Simpson trail. The trial was aired on news stations almost nonstop. Things such as the evidence presented and verdict were not changed in this coverage, however the way the trial was presented was dependent of the way news stations, whichever one it was, saw fit. The cuts and zooms that appeared on the television video capturing the proceedings of the courtroom were different from the actual experience inside the courtroom. Also the replaying of sound clips presented to a TV audience was distinct to the actual courtroom discussions. This caused those watching from the television via news stations to have different perception of reality than the actual reality of the trial. Depending on what news stations this perception could be altered in number of ways from the actual reality of the trial. Due to this fact, people in actuality cannot distinguish between the version of the trail presented to them on their televisions, which is a false reality, and the actual reality of the trial. This fact can have a number of implications, however these implications will not be discussed. This post is meant just to reveal the existence of hyperreality in media and its effect on news. Hyperreality is especially prevalent in news coverage similar to the O.J. Simpson trail such as Princess Diana’s death, the Casey Anthony trail, and even the recent mystery of flight 370. Hyperreality usually occurs in news that receive 24 hour coverage as such coverage provides more than mere reports, but rather creates its own reality of the event being covered.
